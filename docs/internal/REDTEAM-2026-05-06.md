# Citeback Red Team Report
**Date:** 2026-05-06  
**Analyst:** Adversarial Red Team  
**Target:** Citeback.com — reputation system, campaign funding model, verification infrastructure  
**Classification:** Internal — Pre-Launch  

---

## Executive Summary

Citeback's threat model is unusually sophisticated for an early-stage project. The TEE financial architecture is sound. The C2PA verification layer is real. The graduated tier system shows genuine design thinking. But several attack surfaces are either unmitigated or defended by mechanisms that don't exist yet in code. This report finds **three critical vulnerabilities** that could destroy platform credibility before launch, three high-severity issues that require pre-launch mitigations, and six medium-severity issues that can be addressed post-launch with priority scheduling.

The most dangerous attacks are not technical. They are social engineering attacks that exploit the gap between human review procedures that are *described* and human review procedures that are *implemented*.

## Attack 1: Sybil Factory — Farming Tier 1 at Scale

### Attack Description
An adversary creates 50 email accounts using temp-mail services (Guerrilla Mail, 10-minute mail, SimpleLogin). Each account submits exactly 10 camera sightings using publicly available OSM surveillance data — specifically, scraping OpenStreetMap's `surveillance=*` tag for any city with high camera density (London, Chicago, NYC). Each OSM-verified camera submitted as a "new sighting" from 50 accounts = 50 accounts all reaching Tier 1 simultaneously.

Cost: One afternoon. No photos. No in-person verification. Just scraped OSM IDs resubmitted as community sightings.

**Twist:** The attacker doesn't even need to fabricate cameras — they scrape cameras that are *already on Citeback* but in nearby addresses, slightly varied, to farm double-points for "new" cameras. 200 North Main St becomes 200 N. Main Street. The duplicate detection is only as good as its string-matching and coordinate radius.

### Feasibility: 1/5 (trivial)

### Current Defense
- Honeypot field in SightingForm (catches naive bots, not humans)
- Submission goes to moderation queue
- Cross-reference against OSM

### Does It Work?
**No.** The moderation queue is staffed by humans (the founder at launch). At any meaningful scale of attack, the queue becomes a DoS vector against the human reviewer, not a real defense. The OSM cross-reference confirms the camera exists — it does not confirm the submitter has ever been near it. A camera in Chicago can be "sighted" from New Mexico using OSM data.

The sighting form explicitly states "Anonymous, no account, no contact info required." This is a privacy feature that is simultaneously a Sybil enablement feature.

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **Velocity rate-limiting per IP/fingerprint** — cap sightings from a single source at 3/day, 10/week, with CAPTCHA above threshold
2. **Geographic plausibility signal** — not a hard block, but flag sightings where the submitter's approximate location (IP geolocation) is >500 miles from the submitted camera. Queue these for human review with that flag attached
3. **New-camera double-points require photo evidence** — even a non-C2PA photo. The "double points for new cameras" mechanic specifically rewards the high-fraud action. Require at minimum a photo upload (any format) for new camera claims; C2PA upgrades the tier but a photo of *something* adds friction
4. **Submission velocity fingerprinting** — browser fingerprint (canvas, timezone, fonts) to detect single-actor multi-account abuse. Not IP-only; VPNs trivially bypass that

**Post-launch:**
- Stake requirement for Tier 1 promotion (small XMR deposit — even $5 worth — that's slashable on confirmed fraud)
- Vouching requirement: one existing Tier 1 operator must co-sign a new Tier 1 promotion

### Build Priority: **BEFORE LAUNCH** — this is the foundation of the entire reputation system. If Sybil attacks can trivially farm Tier 1, every downstream tier is compromised.

## Attack 2: Fake Camera Submission — OSM Laundering

### Attack Description
**Vector A — Pure fabrication:** Submit cameras that don't exist at all. Since the sighting form is anonymous and goes to a moderation queue, the attacker submits plausible-sounding intersections ("NE corner of MLK Blvd and 5th Ave, ALPR mounted on traffic signal pole"). OSM doesn't have it. Citeback doesn't have it. Human moderator may approve it as a legitimate new sighting.

**Vector B — Address variant farming:** Submit the same real camera 10 times with slight address variations: "200 Main St", "200 N Main St", "200 North Main Street", "200 Main Street NW", "Intersection of Main and 2nd, NW corner", etc. Naive duplicate detection based on address string fails. If coordinates are resolved via Nominatim, coordinate-radius deduplication may catch it — but only if the server checks incoming submissions against the existing database in real time.

**Vector C — OSM data laundering:** The attacker scrapes OSM surveillance tags for real cameras, submits them as "never-been-submitted community sightings." They receive double points because the system has no record. The camera is real, the coordinates are real, but the submitter has never been within 1,000 miles of it.

### Feasibility: 1/5 (Vector C trivial, A and B require small effort)

### Current Defense
- Nominatim geocoding confirms the address exists
- "Cross-referenced against OSM" per the threat disclosure section
- Moderation queue

### Does It Work?
**Partially, not fully.** Nominatim confirms an address exists in the real world — it says nothing about whether a surveillance camera is mounted there. The OSM cross-reference helps for cameras already in OSM, but Citeback's *entire value proposition for new cameras* is discovering infrastructure not yet in OSM. You cannot use OSM to verify what isn't in OSM.

Vector B (address variants) relies entirely on how tight the coordinate deduplication radius is. If it's set to 50 meters, submitting addresses that geocode to the same block will be caught. If it's set at 200 meters, only exact duplicates are flagged.

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **Coordinate-based deduplication with configurable radius** — 30-meter radius for urban areas, 100-meter for suburban, 500-meter for rural. Flag any new submission within that radius of an existing entry as a potential duplicate, require human review
2. **OSM check runs on coordinates, not address string** — if submitted coordinates are within 50 meters of an existing OSM surveillance node, flag as likely duplicate
3. **Minimum photo required for double-points** — any photo, even non-C2PA. Makes Vector A and C harder because you need something visual

**Post-launch:**
- After sufficient data, ML-based duplicate detection on coordinates + camera type + description embedding

### Build Priority: **BEFORE LAUNCH** — coordinate deduplication is a database query, not a system. This should be day-one.

## Attack 3: C2PA Spoofing — The Cryptographic Weak Points

### Attack Description
The C2PA defense is genuinely strong — but has specific exploitable failure modes.

**Vector A — Pre-signing GPS manipulation:** C2PA signs the GPS coordinates embedded *by the camera app at capture time.* But on Android, apps that request location permission *before* shutter can receive a spoofed GPS coordinate if the device is running a GPS spoofing app (Fake GPS, Mock Locations). The C2PA signature will be valid — it will cryptographically prove the coordinates it was given. If those coordinates were spoofed at the OS level, C2PA cannot detect it.

**Vector B — Non-C2PA device fallback exploitation:** The current SKILL.md recommends Truepic and a handful of C2PA-capable apps. The vast majority of users will not have these apps. What is the actual fallback path for camera sightings submitted without C2PA? If the answer is "human moderation," that's a queue-flooding vector. If the answer is "no double points," that's fine — but it must be strictly enforced.

**Vector C — C2PA app compromise:** Truepic is a third-party app. If an attacker finds a way to manipulate Truepic's signing key, or spoofs the Truepic environment at the app layer, all C2PA certificates from that app become suspect. This is low probability but catastrophic if it occurs.

**Vector D — Old photo, new location claim:** Take a C2PA-signed photo from a real C2PA camera at real coordinates. Submit it as a milestone proof for a campaign in a completely different city. C2PA proves the photo is genuine — but doesn't prove it's of the claimed surveillance target. Server validates signature + GPS, confirms the photo is authentic, milestone clears. But the photo is of a billboard in Denver being submitted as proof of a billboard in Albuquerque.

### Feasibility
- Vector A: 2/5 (requires GPS spoofing app + technical setup)
- Vector B: 1/5 (just use a regular phone)
- Vector C: 4/5 (requires app-level compromise, very difficult)
- Vector D: 2/5 (straightforward if you have any C2PA device)

### Current Defense
- "Server validates signature + GPS coordinates automatically"
- C2PA cryptographic standard backed by Adobe, Google, Microsoft
- Content Credentials verification

### Does It Work?
**Vector A: No** — GPS spoofing at the OS level is undetectable by C2PA. The signature is valid because the spoofed coordinates were fed to the app before signing.

**Vector B: Not defined** — no fallback policy is visible in the current code or documentation.

**Vector C: Yes, with caveats** — the signature would become invalid if the key is compromised; Content Credentials infrastructure would revoke it. But there's a window between compromise and revocation.

**Vector D: This is a critical gap.** The server validates that the C2PA photo is authentic. It cannot validate that the photo is *of what the operator claims*. GPS coordinates can confirm the photo was taken near a location — but a billboard photo at 35.0844°N, 106.6504°W proves nothing about a billboard campaign in a different city. **The milestone proof verification for physical deliverables has no semantic layer.**

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **Vector D — Mandatory GPS coordinate matching for milestone proofs:** When an operator submits a C2PA photo as milestone proof, server must compare the photo's GPS coordinates against the campaign's registered location. Accept only if within a configurable radius (e.g., 1 mile for billboard, 100 meters for camera hardware installation). This is critical.
2. **Vector B — Explicit fallback policy:** Document and enforce that non-C2PA photos receive no double-points for camera reputation *and* cannot satisfy physical-deliverable milestones. C2PA must be mandatory for anything money moves on.
3. **Vector A — Treat GPS spoofing risk honestly:** C2PA GPS is not tamper-proof at the OS layer. For high-value milestone proofs, require multiple independent verifiers to confirm physical presence (3-of-3 consensus from expert directory, or operator's own documentation trail). Add explicit language to the terms that GPS spoofing is a reportable violation.

**Post-launch:**
- Cross-reference photo GPS coordinates against publicly known addresses in the milestone plan (requires structured milestone data with registered lat/lng)
- Incentivize expert directory verifiers to spot-check high-value milestones in person

### Build Priority: **BEFORE LAUNCH (Vector D is critical)** — milestone proof validation is the primary anti-fraud mechanism for fund release. A semantic gap here means money moves based on proof of *nothing specific*.

## Attack 4: The Long Con — Reputation Investment Fraud

### Attack Description
An attacker invests 6–12 months building legitimate reputation. They run 3 successful Tier 1 campaigns (under $1,000 each) with real execution — filing genuine small FOIA requests, posting legitimate billboard photos. Cost: ~$3,000 all-in, including any platform fees and actual deliverable costs.

After establishing Tier 2 standing, they run 2 legitimate Tier 2 campaigns (~$2,500 each). Total investment: ~$8,000 and one year of time.

They obtain community vouching from two Tier 3 operators (using the vouching ring attack, see Attack 7, or through genuine relationships). They get promoted to Tier 3.

They propose a $25,000 campaign — "FOIA litigation for Flock Safety contract disclosure in [major city], milestone 1: file complaint, milestone 2: discovery, milestone 3: judgment or settlement." It's compelling. Community funds it. Tranche 1 (~$5,000) releases upon filing complaint.

They file a real complaint in small claims court (costs $50). Collect Tranche 1. Go dark.

**ROI on the attack:** $5,000 collected minus ~$8,000 invested = net loss. But the attacker's goal may not be financial. A well-funded adversary (surveillance vendor) would happily spend $8,000 to run a successful exit scam, create a damaging news story ("Citeback operator disappears with $5,000"), and undermine donor trust in the platform.

The real danger: they extract the maximum first tranche before disappearing, or they conduct the attack repeatedly via slightly different identities after the first burn.

### Feasibility: 3/5 (requires significant time investment)

### Current Defense
- Identity verification for operators
- OFAC screening
- Milestone-based fund release
- 48-hour challenge window on disbursements
- Community track record requirement

### Does It Work?
**Partially.** Identity verification and OFAC screening mean the operator is a real, identified person — not anonymous. This creates real legal exposure for the attacker. However:
- Small claims court complaint is a real filing and will clear milestone 1
- First tranche release is largely automatic once proof is submitted
- The 48-hour challenge window assumes community members are watching — there's no guarantee
- Recovery after the fact requires legal action against an identified person, which is slow and expensive

The TEE architecture means funds can't be clawed back once released. This is a feature (censorship resistance) and a bug (no recovery mechanism).

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **Tier 3 minimum time-lock:** No Tier 3 campaign can accept disbursements until the operator has been active for 365 days (not 60). Time investment by attacker increases to nearly 2 years minimum for meaningful extraction.
2. **Vouching accountability — vouchers are co-liable:** When two Tier 3 operators vouch for a Tier 2→Tier 3 promotion, make them explicitly acknowledge that a successful fraud campaign against the vouchee reduces their own reputation score by a meaningful amount. Vouchers have skin in the game.
3. **Milestone tranche caps:** First tranche for Tier 3 campaigns capped at 20% of total goal (not 33%). Requires successful completion of milestone 1 *and* a 7-day community challenge window (not 48 hours) before first Tier 3 disbursement.
4. **On-chain reputation as collateral:** For Tier 3+, require an XMR/ZANO deposit equal to 10% of campaign goal held in escrow inside the TEE. Returned upon successful campaign completion; burned on confirmed fraud. This is the economic deterrent.

**Post-launch:**
- Cross-campaign pattern analysis: same operator running similar campaign structures repeatedly, always extracting first tranche then going quiet, should trigger automatic flagging
- Donor recourse: small reserve fund (from platform fees) that can compensate documented fraud victims up to a defined cap, creating partial insurance without undermining custody model

### Build Priority: **BEFORE LAUNCH for tranche cap** — the 20% first-tranche rule is a configuration, not an engineering effort. The time-lock and collateral require more design work.

## Attack 5: MuckRock URL Fraud — The Semantic Mismatch Attack

### Attack Description
This is one of the most elegant attacks in the threat model. It exploits a fundamental gap between URL validation and content validation.

**The attack:** File a real FOIA through MuckRock. Request, say, "How many staples were purchased by the Denver Police Department in 2024." It's a valid FOIA. MuckRock assigns it a public URL: `https://www.muckrock.com/foi/denver-2024-XXXXX/`. The URL resolves. The server pings it, gets 200 OK, milestone clears.

The campaign description says: "FOIA filing to force disclosure of all Flock Safety ALPR deployment contracts and surveillance sharing agreements with federal agencies." The URL says: "How many staples."

The server has no way to know what the FOIA actually requests. It confirmed the URL exists. Milestone cleared. Tranche released.

**Escalation:** For a $25,000 campaign, the operator might file a real, relevant FOIA — but frame the campaign as if the FOIA is the *entire* campaign rather than just one milestone step. The remaining milestones ("follow up on appeal," "prepare for litigation") are described vaguely and can be satisfied with trivial actions.

### Feasibility: 1/5 (trivial — just file the wrong FOIA)

### Current Defense
- "Server pings URL to confirm it exists" — explicitly described
- Human review of campaign proposals

### Does It Work?
**No, for URL validation.** A URL ping confirms HTTP 200. It does not confirm:
- The FOIA is about what the campaign claims
- The FOIA is filed for the right agency
- The FOIA request text matches the campaign description

Human review at proposal stage *might* catch this if reviewers verify the MuckRock URL content before approving the milestone. But: (1) the URL might not exist yet at proposal time, (2) reviewers may not check content carefully on every disbursement request, (3) there's no mechanism to link a specific MuckRock request to a specific campaign ID in a verifiable way.

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **Require MuckRock URL at proposal time, not at milestone time:** Operators should either (a) already have the FOIA filed and link it in the proposal, OR (b) explicitly commit to a specific agency, jurisdiction, and topic that will be verified against the actual MuckRock filing. The proposal text becomes a verifiable commitment.
2. **Content hash commitment:** At proposal approval, operator provides a text description of what the FOIA will request. At milestone submission, a human reviewer (or community) compares the actual MuckRock request text against the committed description. If they don't match, milestone fails.
3. **Require MuckRock request body accessible on page:** Most MuckRock FOIAs display the actual request text publicly. Add to the server-side validation: scrape the MuckRock page for the request text, extract it, require the operator to confirm it matches. Doesn't have to be automatic — surface it to the human reviewer as part of the milestone approval flow.
4. **Same analysis applies to CourtListener and OpenStates:** Court case numbers and legislation URLs must be matched against campaign-committed case descriptions.

### Build Priority: **BEFORE LAUNCH** — this is a trivially exploitable gap in every FOIA-based campaign. It requires a procedural change, not engineering. Add "reviewer must confirm URL content matches campaign description" to the milestone approval checklist.

## Attack 6: Credential Forgery for Tier 4 — Bar Number Laundering

### Attack Description
**Vector A — Inactive attorney:** Look up a real attorney who has been inactive for 5+ years (state bars publish disciplinary records and inactive status). Use their real bar number. The AI verification checks the bar number against the public bar database — it resolves. The attorney exists. The bar number is valid. The system has no way to verify the submitter *is* that attorney.

**Vector B — Deceased attorney:** Search obituaries + bar records for deceased attorneys whose bar numbers haven't been formally surrendered. Submit their credentials.

**Vector C — Identity theft of active attorney:** More brazen. Use a real, active attorney's bar number. If there's no live verification with the attorney (email, call, document signing), the system can't distinguish the real attorney from an impersonator.

**Vector D — Fake organizational credentials:** For Tier 4's "org registration" requirement — submit a real Wyoming LLC registration number that belongs to a dormant company. Wyoming LLCs are $102 to form and persist for years. An attacker can form a legitimate Wyoming LLC, use it to clear Tier 4, run a campaign, and dissolve it. The liability shield from a dissolved LLC makes recovery difficult.

### Feasibility
- Vector A/B: 2/5 (requires research, no special skills)
- Vector C: 3/5 (requires knowing a real attorney's name + bar number)
- Vector D: 1/5 (trivial — $102 LLC + 20 minutes)

### Current Defense
- "AI verification against public bar databases"
- "Institutional credentials verified (bar number, org registration)"
- OFAC screening

### Does It Work?
**AI verification confirms the credential exists, not that the submitter holds it.** This is a fundamental gap. OFAC screening confirms the operator isn't on a sanctions list — it doesn't confirm they're who they claim to be.

Vector D (Wyoming LLC) is essentially not a defense at all. Wyoming LLCs require no disclosed officers in many configurations, and $102 is a rounding error for an adversary running a $100,000 campaign.

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **Bar number verification requires attorney consent:** Send a verification request to the bar's registered email address for that attorney, or require a signed letter on firm letterhead. AI lookup is not verification — it's a lookup.
2. **Video call or notarized attestation for Tier 4:** Tier 4 campaigns involve up to $100,000. A 15-minute video call to confirm identity against government ID is not unreasonable. Document and retain the recording.
3. **Entity must be active at disbursement time:** Verify organizational registration status at the time of each disbursement, not just at onboarding. A dissolved LLC should block disbursement pending review.
4. **Bar status at disbursement:** Re-verify bar status (active/inactive/suspended) at each milestone disbursement for attorney-credentialed operators. Bar suspensions are public record.

**Post-launch:**
- Law firm verification via Law360, Avvo, or direct firm website check (does the attorney's name appear on the firm's public website?)
- Partnership with state bars for direct credential lookup APIs (long-term)

### Build Priority: **BEFORE LAUNCH** — Tier 4 is the highest-value target. $100,000 campaigns with unverified credentials is unacceptable. Implement consent-based verification before Tier 4 is enabled.

## Attack 7: Vouching Rings — Distributed Reputation Laundering

### Attack Description
Three individuals coordinate:
- Person A runs 3 Tier 1 campaigns (legitimate small campaigns). Gets Tier 2.
- Person B runs 3 Tier 1 campaigns (legitimate). Gets Tier 2.  
- Person C runs 3 Tier 1 campaigns (legitimate). Gets Tier 2.

All three then run mediocre Tier 2 campaigns — the campaigns "complete" but barely. 90%+ completion rate is met by technicality.

Person A gets vouched by existing Tier 3 operators (somehow — see below). Gets Tier 3. Now vouches for B and C. B vouches for A and C. C vouches for A and B.

**The bootstrapping problem:** How do the first members of a vouching ring get Tier 3 status? Options:
1. Exploit the founder/early operator trust during the platform's early days when Tier 3 is lightly populated
2. Legitimately earn Tier 3 (which costs real time and money) and then use that legitimacy as a ring-leader
3. Wait until the platform has enough Tier 3 operators that two unrelated-looking accounts can be compromised or socially engineered

The ring can now run Tier 3 campaigns. If the ring operates across 3 different names/entities, attribution requires cross-account analytics that the current system doesn't have.

### Feasibility: 3/5 (requires coordination and some legitimate investment)

### Current Defense
- "Community vouching from 2 Tier 3 operators" for Tier 3 promotion
- Track record requirements

### Does It Work?
**No systematic defense against coordinated rings.** The vouching system assumes vouchers are independent actors who have assessed the vouchee's work independently. It has no mechanism to detect when vouchers are part of the same social/financial network as the vouchee.

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **Vouching graph analysis:** When a vouching relationship is submitted, check whether the vouchee and vouchers share any prior campaign interactions, were onboarded within the same time window, or have submitted from similar geographic/IP signatures. Flag concentrated clusters for human review.
2. **Voucher diversity requirement:** The two Tier 3 vouchers must have been active on the platform for at least 180 days longer than the vouchee. This prevents fast-bootstrapped rings.
3. **Vouching history is public:** All vouch relationships should be visible in the public action log. Community can self-police by spotting dense vouching clusters.
4. **Maximum vouches per operator per 90 days:** A single Tier 3 operator can vouch for no more than 2 people per quarter. Prevents one trusted operator from being socially engineered into mass-vouching.

**Post-launch:**
- Graph centrality analysis on the vouching network — high betweenness centrality nodes (one person vouching for many) are red flags
- Require vouchers to have completed at least one campaign *after* the vouchee was promoted (creates ongoing accountability)

### Build Priority: **POST-LAUNCH** — vouching rings require coordination and take time to develop. The platform won't have enough Tier 3 operators at launch for rings to form immediately. But the public vouching log and maximum vouches/quarter should be **in the initial design**.

## Attack 8: Vague Milestone Manipulation — Semantic Escape Hatches

### Attack Description
Operator proposes a campaign with milestone 1 defined as: "Advocacy activities conducted and community outreach completed." Gets approved. Runs some Twitter posts. Submits a screenshot as proof. Claims milestone complete. No C2PA required — no physical deliverable. No FOIA URL. Just "activities."

Or more subtle: Milestone 1 is "Preliminary legal research and strategy development completed." Produces a 3-page Word document with generic legal research copied from public sources. Submits it as proof. Milestone clears. Tranche 1 releases.

The attack exploits the gap between specific, verifiable milestones (FOIA filed, billboard installed, court case number) and vague, unverifiable milestones (research done, advocacy conducted, planning completed).

### Feasibility: 1/5 (trivial — just write vague milestones)

### Current Defense
- "Operator lays out a plan, tranches unlock as milestones are verified"
- Campaign review during proposal stage
- Community challenge window

### Does It Work?
**Depends entirely on proposal review quality.** If reviewers are diligent, vague milestones get rejected at proposal time. If reviewers are overloaded, rushed, or not using a structured rubric, vague milestones slip through. The community challenge window is only effective if someone is watching and knows what a concrete milestone looks like.

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **Milestone type taxonomy with required proof format:** Define a closed list of milestone types. Each type has a required proof format:
   - `foia_filing` → MuckRock URL required
   - `court_filing` → CourtListener case number required  
   - `billboard_installed` → C2PA photo with GPS required
   - `legislation_introduced` → OpenStates URL required
   - `legal_research` → **not a valid standalone milestone** (internal deliverable, not verifiable)
   - `community_outreach` → **not a valid milestone type** (unverifiable)
2. **Milestone descriptions must include "proof will be" statement:** Every milestone in the proposal must explicitly state what evidence will be submitted. If it can't be stated concisely, it's not a milestone — it's a task.
3. **Reviewer checklist:** Published rubric for proposal review that includes "Is every milestone tied to a verifiable, external deliverable?"

### Build Priority: **BEFORE LAUNCH** — this is a proposal review procedure, not engineering. Write the milestone taxonomy and add it to the proposal form as required fields before any Tier 3+ campaigns are accepted.

## Attack 9: View Key Deception — The Wallet Bait-and-Switch

### Attack Description
**Vector A — Wrong wallet:** Operator publishes a view key for wallet A. Tells donors the wallet address is wallet A. Secretly directs donors to wallet B (the real collection wallet) by publishing wallet B's address publicly while publishing wallet A's view key. Donors verify the view key against wallet A — it shows a growing balance. Wallet A is funded legitimately to look convincing. But the operator's real ask sends funds to wallet B, which has no view key published and cannot be independently audited.

**Already-spent view key deception:** Operator publishes a view key for a wallet that had $10,000 at some point (perhaps a previous campaign). The view key shows historical balance. Donors don't check whether the funds were already spent. The current balance shown depends on how the XMR view key tool works — some tools show all-time received, not current balance.

**Vector B — Fake view key:** Publish a made-up string as a "view key." Most donors won't verify. Those who try might get a confusing error and assume they're doing it wrong.

### Feasibility
- Vector A: 2/5 (requires understanding XMR wallet mechanics)
- Vector B: 1/5 (trivial — copy-paste a fake string)

### Current Defense
- View key published publicly
- "Anyone can verify the balance in real time"
- TEE architecture — campaign wallets created by TEE, view keys published by TEE

### Does It Work?
**The TEE architecture fixes this for TEE-managed campaigns.** If the TEE creates the wallet and publishes the view key, the operator never controls the wallet address or view key — the TEE does. This eliminates Vector A for TEE campaigns.

**Critical gap:** What about campaigns during the pre-TEE launch phase? The architecture doc states TEE is the target but notes "Phala or Marlin for launch." If TEE is not deployed at launch, these attacks are fully viable.

Also: "When goal is hit, site marks FUNDED and removes wallet address from the page." This is a UX feature that *also* hides the wallet from new visitors, making post-funding verification harder. A donor who donates after the address is removed can't verify their donation hit the right wallet.

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **No live campaigns without TEE wallet creation.** If TEE isn't ready, no Tier 3+ campaigns. Period. Tier 1/2 campaigns could potentially run with escrow-lite arrangements, but the wallet creation must be TEE-managed for anything above $5,000.
2. **Never remove wallet address from the page** — only mark it FUNDED. The address should be permanently visible for auditability. Removing it serves UX at the expense of verification.
3. **Publish both address and view key in the same signed attestation** — proof that the view key corresponds to the published address. This requires the TEE to produce this proof at wallet creation.

### Build Priority: **CRITICAL — NO TEE = NO TRUST MODEL.** The entire financial architecture rests on the TEE. Launching without it means the operator controls funds, which contradicts the core promise. This is not a nice-to-have.

## Attack 10: Account Marketplace — Reputation Laundering by Transfer

### Attack Description
An attacker or team builds legitimate Tier 2 or Tier 3 accounts through genuine campaigns. Once promoted, they sell or transfer operational control of the account to a bad actor. The buyer inherits the reputation, the track record, and the trust — built by someone else.

Price point: A legitimate Tier 3 account with a clean 2-year history might be worth $10,000–$50,000 to the right buyer (a surveillance vendor running a counter-operation, a bad actor wanting to extract a large campaign, a nation-state).

The original operator could claim they simply "hired someone" to manage their campaigns, making the transfer look like staff turnover rather than account sale.

### Feasibility: 3/5 (requires a willing original account holder)

### Current Defense
- Identity verification at onboarding
- OFAC screening

### Does It Work?
**No.** Identity verification confirms who created the account. It does not monitor for behavioral change over time. A Tier 3 account that suddenly changes communication patterns, runs an uncharacteristic campaign, or requests unusual milestone types should be flagged — but there's no mechanism for this.

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **Behavioral continuity monitoring:** Track consistent identifiers across an operator's history (communication style, campaign types, geographic patterns). Sudden shifts trigger manual review before milestone disbursement.
2. **Re-verification at tier transitions and large campaigns:** Any campaign above $10,000 requires re-verification of operator identity (not just onboarding — again, at proposal time).
3. **Non-transferability clause in terms:** Explicit TOS clause that operator accounts are non-transferable. Provides legal hook if account sale is later discovered. Doesn't prevent it, but creates liability.
4. **Pattern matching against known accounts:** If the same operator is suddenly proposing a campaign type they've never run before, with a much larger budget, from what appears to be a different device/location — flag it.

**Post-launch:**
- Session-level behavioral biometrics (typing patterns, navigation, time-of-day) as soft signals — not hard blocks, but anomaly indicators

### Build Priority: **POST-LAUNCH** — account marketplace attacks require significant investment and take time. TOS non-transferability clause is a pre-launch no-brainer. Behavioral monitoring is post-launch.

## Attack 11: Score Optimization — Gaming the Threshold

### Attack Description
An attacker reads the tier requirements carefully:
- Tier 1: 10 verified cameras
- Tier 2: 3 completed Tier 1 campaigns, 90%+ completion rate, 60+ days

They don't try to maximize contribution — they try to minimize cost to hit these exact thresholds.

For Tier 1: Submit exactly 10 camera sightings. Prioritize OSM-confirmed cameras to maximize approval probability. Done.

For Tier 2: Run exactly 3 Tier 1 campaigns at minimum allowed size ($1 each, if the system allows). Each campaign has exactly one milestone that is trivially satisfiable. Complete all three. Completion rate = 100%. Wait 60 days. Apply for Tier 2.

The campaigns might be entirely genuine — small, low-cost FOIA filings for public records that nobody will actually do anything with. Technically completed. Tier 2 access earned for ~$200 and 60 days.

### Feasibility: 2/5 (requires patience and some nominal campaign costs)

### Current Defense
- Community review
- Completion rate tracking
- Peer assessment

### Does It Work?
**Marginally.** The thresholds as described create exact optimization targets. Someone gaming the system will hit every threshold precisely and no more. The 90%+ completion rate is easy to game if you only run campaigns you're certain you can complete minimally.

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **Minimum campaign scope requirement:** Tier 1 campaigns must request a minimum of $100 and have at least one externally verifiable deliverable. Campaigns with no external proof requirement should not count toward tier advancement.
2. **Completion *quality* not just completion *rate*:** Track whether campaigns generated community engagement (donors, public comments, challenge window activity). A campaign with 0 donors and 0 community interaction is a red flag for threshold-gaming.
3. **Fuzzy thresholds with human review:** Instead of "exactly 3 campaigns → Tier 2," require 3 campaigns **reviewed by a human reviewer who confirms they represent genuine contribution**. This adds friction to pure threshold-gaming.

### Build Priority: **POST-LAUNCH** — a determined attacker who games thresholds minimally is still providing some genuine value (3 real FOIA filings, even trivial ones). This is lower priority than attacks that provide zero value.

## Attack 12: The Empty Campaign — Funded but Never Executed

### Attack Description
Operator creates a compelling campaign: "FOIA litigation against [city] police department for ShotSpotter contracts — 3 milestones, 120 days." Donors fund it. Goal is $5,000 (Tier 1 range — low enough to not require milestone-based release).

Wait. Campaign goal is hit. Platform marks FUNDED. Operator receives funds. Never executes. Posts a fake C2PA photo as proof (or simply claims the milestone was "preliminary research," which has no required proof format — see Attack 8).

For campaigns that are **not** milestone-based (Tier 1/2 without required milestones), there may be no disbursement gating at all. The operator gets the money and disappears.

### Feasibility: 2/5 (requires legitimate Tier 1/2 status)

### Current Defense
- "No platform fee. Self-funded." — doesn't help donors
- Operator identity on file for legal action
- Milestone-based release for Tier 3/4

### Does It Work?
**For Tier 1/2: Depends entirely on whether milestones are required.** If Tier 1 campaigns release funds on goal completion with no milestone gating, this attack is trivially executable. The current architecture says "Tier 3/4 require milestone-based fund release" — implying Tier 1/2 may not.

**This is the most ambiguous point in the model.** If Tier 1/2 campaigns release funds directly upon goal completion, they are structurally identical to a traditional crowdfunding scam.

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **ALL campaigns require at least one milestone** — even Tier 1. The milestone can be simple (FOIA filed = URL required, billboard installed = C2PA photo required), but there must be something that blocks automatic disbursement.
2. **Challenge window for all tiers:** Even Tier 1 campaigns should have a 24-48 hour community challenge window before disbursement. This creates a public record and a deterrent.
3. **Donor recourse fund:** A small percentage of platform fees (~0.5%) goes into a community-governed recourse fund. Documented fraud victims (operators who never execute with funds disbursed) can petition for compensation up to a defined cap. This doesn't require reversing transactions — it's insurance from reserves.

### Build Priority: **CRITICAL BEFORE LAUNCH** — requiring at least one milestone for all tiers is a policy decision that must be made before campaigns go live.

## Attack 13: Geographic Fraud — Remote OSM Laundering for Reputation

### Attack Description
As described in the threat model: User in New Mexico submits 10 cameras in New York City using OSM data. OSM has those cameras tagged. They exist. The Citeback system cross-references OSM, confirms the cameras exist, awards reputation points — potentially double points if they weren't previously in the Citeback database.

The submitter has never been to New York. The cameras are real — but the *verification contribution* is fake. They're not adding new knowledge to the surveillance map; they're laundering public data into reputation points.

This attack is particularly effective because:
1. OSM has millions of surveillance-tagged nodes globally
2. The submission form is anonymous with no location requirement for submitters
3. There is no mechanism to require proximity to a submitted camera

### Feasibility: 1/5 (trivial — just scrape OSM tags)

### Current Defense
- OSM cross-reference (confirms camera exists, not submitter's presence)
- Moderation queue
- Honeypot field (irrelevant here)

### Does It Work?
**No, for the stated attack.** The OSM cross-reference is functioning exactly as intended — it's the problem, not the solution here, because it *validates* the submission without validating the submitter's knowledge.

### Mitigation
**Before launch:**
1. **Zero reputation points for OSM-confirmed cameras submitted via text-only form.** If a camera is already in OSM and the submitter provides no C2PA photo, they get zero points. Full stop. The value they're adding is zero — OSM already knows about it. Reputation points should reward *new* knowledge.
2. **Double points only for C2PA-verified new cameras** — not for new-camera text submissions. The double-points mechanic is specifically valuable and specifically gameable. Lock it to C2PA.
3. **For confirmed/disputed camera verification (the 3-of-3 consensus):** Require verifiers to submit at minimum a non-C2PA photo with plausible EXIF metadata (timestamp within the last 30 days, location roughly matching). This isn't proof-positive but raises the bar from pure text gaming.

### Build Priority: **BEFORE LAUNCH** — the reputation point system is the foundation of operator trust. If it's gameable via OSM scraping from a couch, Tier 1 operators may all be frauds from day one.

## What's Actually Working

This isn't all criticism. Several design choices are genuinely strong:

1. **TEE architecture** — if implemented, is a real solution to custody risk, not theater
2. **Identity verification for operators (not donors)** — the right tradeoff. Operators are accountable; donors are not
3. **C2PA for physical deliverables** — real technology with real guarantees (within its scope)
4. **OFAC screening** — appropriate for XMR handling at this scale
5. **Graduated fee structure** — economically rational and doesn't front-load operator friction
6. **Public view keys** — genuine transparency mechanism, just needs to survive the "remove on funded" UX decision
7. **48-hour challenge window** — good design, needs extension for Tier 3+ (7 days minimum)

The platform's instincts are correct. The gaps are almost entirely in implementation detail and policy, not in architectural design. Most of the pre-launch mitigations are procedural rather than engineering-intensive. The most critical one (TEE) is already planned.
