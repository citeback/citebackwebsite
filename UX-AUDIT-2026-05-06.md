# Citeback UX Audit — 2026-05-06

**Conducted by:** UX Research / Red-Team Simulation  
**Source reviewed:** `/deflect/src/` — Hero, TrustFAQ, SightingForm, CampaignList, CampaignCard, CampaignModal, Footer, Nav, App.jsx  
**Method:** Five user persona simulations + cross-cutting analysis

---

## Persona 1: The Curious Newcomer

> 35-year-old, heard about ALPR cameras from a friend. Found Citeback via a tweet. No prior context.

### Journey

1. **Lands on homepage.** Sees the hero headline — but the key words are **blacked out** with a cinematic redaction animation. On first load, "surveillance", "everyone", and "fighting" are hidden. The animation slowly plays them off, but timing varies and on a slow scroll they may still be blacked out.
2. **10-second test:** The headline *after* reveal reads "The surveillance state watches everyone. We fund the people fighting back." That's clear enough — but the **sub-headline explains funding with Monero/Zano** immediately. Most 35-year-olds don't know what Monero is. The phrase "privacy cryptocurrencies that leave no on-chain link between sender and recipient" is technically accurate but **opaque jargon** to a newcomer.
3. **Two CTAs visible:** "See Campaigns" and "Run a Campaign." The newcomer clicks "See Campaigns."
4. **Campaigns page:** All campaigns are marked **"Pre-Launch" with "Wallet pending."** Progress bars show 0%. There's no explanation *on the campaigns page* for why nothing is funded. The "Wallet pending" label is cryptic to a non-technical user — they likely interpret it as "broken" or "fake."
5. **Clicking a campaign card:** Opens CampaignModal. The pre-launch state shows "Signal Interest" with a ThumbsUp button. The only follow-up instruction is "Bookmark this page and check back." **There is no email signup, no mailing list, no Nostr subscription, no Discord.** A newcomer has no way to know when it goes live.
6. **Map:** If they navigate to `/map`, they see ~92,000 ALPR cameras plotted (from EFF/OpenStreetMap data). This is impressive. But there is **no explanation on the map page** of what they're looking at, where the data came from, or what "community-reported" vs. baseline data means.
7. **Finding the sighting form:** It's buried under the "More" dropdown in nav as "Report a Sighting." A newcomer who wants to participate actively may not discover it.

### Friction Points

- **Redaction gimmick:** Clever to techies; confusing and mildly alarming to newcomers ("why is the text blacked out?"). The auto-reveal animation is 6 seconds — users who scroll past the fold before it completes never see the headline.
- **Monero/Zano assumption in the hero paragraph:** The very first explanatory paragraph mentions XMR and ZANO. Newcomers skip this or don't understand it.
- **Platform stat: "$0 Total raised to date"** — displayed prominently in the sidebar stats. To a newcomer this signals either a brand-new platform or a failing one. There's no context that this is intentional/pre-launch.
- **"4/10 launch prerequisites cleared"** — This means nothing without context. No tooltip or link explaining what the prerequisites are from the hero stats panel.
- **No onboarding path:** There's no "New here? Start here" prompt. The page drops users directly into a complex political/technical product.
- **The AI chat button** (fixed bottom-right: "💬 Ask Citeback AI") is the best implicit onboarding tool and it's mostly invisible — no label explaining what it does.
- **What is Citeback actually asking me to do?** The platform has no single clear primary action for a newcomer. Campaign funding, camera reporting, running a campaign, governance — it's unclear where a new user fits.

### Unanswered Questions

- "What is an ALPR?"  (The ALPRExplainer component exists on the homepage but it's below the fold — newcomers likely don't scroll that far on first visit)
- "Why can't I donate right now?"
- "Is this legal?"
- "Who runs this?"
- "Why Monero specifically — isn't that for criminals?"
- "How do I follow this if I want to donate later?"

### Overall Impression: **Skeptical → likely bounces**

The visual design is sharp and the mission is clear after 10 seconds, but the "0 raised," pre-launch everything, and crypto-first framing cause a newcomer to question legitimacy. No re-engagement path means they leave and don't come back.

## Persona 2: The Privacy Activist

> EFF member, knows ALPRs, skeptical of new platforms. First move: audit it.

### Journey

1. **Privacy Policy:** Found in footer nav and in the footer link row. ✅ Exists. Not audited in this review (component not read), but it's present.
2. **Terms of Use:** Same — present. ✅
3. **TrustFAQ / "How It Works":** Reads the whole thing carefully. Finds it thorough and technically credible. The OFAC gap disclosure is unusually candid ("we cannot screen anonymous donors"). The TEE architecture explanation is solid. The risks section is honest (mentions WireTap/TEE.Fail attacks by name with dates).
4. **GitHub repo:** Linked in multiple places — TrustFAQ pillars, footer, individual FAQ answers. ✅ "github.com/citeback/citebackwebsite" referenced but it's a real test whether that repo is populated.
5. **Security headers:** Cannot be checked from frontend code alone. No CSP or security header declarations visible in the React app itself — headers would be set at the hosting layer (Netlify). **There is no mention of security headers anywhere on the site** — a red flag for a paranoid activist.
6. **Infrastructure disclosure:** The TrustFAQ "Risks" section lists Netlify, Bunny CDN, CARTO, CourtListener, USASpending.gov, Senate LDA. This is genuinely impressive transparency. ✅
7. **Who is the founder?** Not named anywhere. The TrustFAQ says "the founder bootstrapped the system." No PGP key, no Nostr pubkey, no legal name, no jurisdiction-specific registration number. A Wyoming DAO LLC is mentioned but "(formation is a Phase 2 prerequisite)" — **it doesn't exist yet.**
8. **TEE attestation:** The FAQ says "We'll publish a verification guide and one-click checker tool when Phase 2 launches." No current attestation to verify. The activist understands this is pre-launch but notes it's entirely trust-based right now.
9. **Phase 1 banner:** Present at the top of TrustFAQ ✅. Clear about what exists vs. what is planned.

### Red Flags for This User

- **Wyoming DAO LLC is not formed.** The legal entity doesn't exist. The activist knows DAO LLCs can be shell structures. No legal accountability mechanism exists yet.
- **No founder identity.** Anonymous founders are common in crypto but a red flag for a platform handling advocacy funds. "Trust the TEE, not the founder" only works when the TEE is live.
- **TEE is not live.** Every claim about "no human can access keys" is a future promise, not a current reality. The Phase 1 banner acknowledges this, but the framing throughout the site (including the hero) implies it's already in place.
- **"No wallets activate until 10 public prerequisites are verified" (hero micro-text):** What are those prerequisites? There's a LaunchTracker component on the homepage, but it's not linked from the hero — users have to scroll the entire homepage to find it, or navigate to it somehow. The micro-text creates intrigue without resolution.
- **Bunny CDN for fonts** — minor, but a privacy activist will note fonts are not self-hosted.
- **CARTO for map tiles** — IP exposure to a commercial mapping provider.
- **No Tor onion address** — A surveillance-resistance platform without a .onion domain is a notable gap.
- **Monero "view keys" described but not yet published.** No current public auditability.
- **No security.txt file mentioned.**

### What They Trust

- The transparency of the risks and infrastructure disclosures
- Open source claim (if GitHub repo is populated)
- The use of privacy coins (conceptually correct choice)
- The Phase 1 banner — it's not hiding what's unbuilt

### Overall Impression: **Skeptical — watches from a distance**

The architecture is sound on paper. But a privacy activist has seen too many "trustless" systems that turned out to trust plenty. Until the TEE is live, the LLC formed, and the attestation endpoint is published, this is an interesting design document, not a trustworthy platform.

## Persona 3: The Angry Citizen

> Just found a Flock Safety camera on their block. Wants to DO something. Probably came from a local news article or Reddit.

### Journey

1. **Lands on homepage.** Reads "We fund the people fighting back." ✅ Resonates immediately.
2. **Clicks "See Campaigns."** Scans for something relevant. Finds campaigns tagged by location (e.g., New Mexico). The campaign cards show: title, type badge, description snippet, progress bar at 0%, and "Pre-Launch / Wallet pending."
3. **Wants to donate.** Clicks a relevant campaign. Modal opens. Big "Pre-Launch Campaign" banner. CTA is "Signal Interest" — not "Donate."
4. **"Signal Interest" clicked.** Gets a ThumbsUp confirmation. That's it. **No next step. No timeline. No email notification ever.** The text says "Bookmark this page and check back." This is a direct bounce trigger for an action-motivated user. **They came here to do something. The platform says "wait."**
5. **"Who is behind this?"** They look at the footer. "Est. 2026 · New Mexico · United States" in the hero. Footer: no address, no legal name, no phone. GitHub link. Tagline. That's it.
6. **Wants to donate anyway.** Searches for a crypto address. There are none — pre-launch. The modal explains why, but the explanation requires understanding TEE enclaves and Wyoming DAO LLCs. An angry citizen's patience for this is approximately zero.
7. **Nav "More" → "Frontline Funds":** Not tested in code but present. This could be the real CTA for this user — existing nonprofits doing related work they can donate to right now. **This is a critical alternative action path that is buried in the "More" dropdown.**

### Friction Points

- **Zero actionable donation path.** Every campaign shows $0 raised, wallet pending, "Signal Interest." A user who came to give money has nowhere to give it.
- **"Signal Interest" is a terrible CTA for an angry citizen.** It reads as "fill in our survey." It should read: "Get in line — be first to donate when we go live."
- **No timeline for wallet activation.** "Phase 2" with no date. "4/10 prerequisites cleared" with no explanation.
- **"Who is behind this?" is unanswered.** An angry citizen asking "is this legit?" gets no answer beyond the design quality and GitHub link. No human name, no organization, no office.
- **Frontline Funds (existing orgs to donate to right now) is hidden.** This is the single best CTA for an impatient motivated user and it's in the "More" dropdown. It should be surfaced on the homepage and campaigns page.
- **No "share this with your neighbors" path.** An angry citizen wants to mobilize. The Share button on campaigns copies a link but there's no framing around community organizing.

### Clear Action Available?

**No.** The angry citizen gets: signal interest, browse the map, or leave. That's a failure for this persona's needs.

### Overall Impression: **Bounces — frustrated**

The platform is in exactly the wrong stage for this persona. They need to donate or act *now*. The pre-launch state with no timeline and no alternative action path results in a bounce. The Frontline Funds page is the escape hatch but it's invisible.

## Persona 4: The Camera Spotter

> Drives around their city spotting surveillance cameras. Wants to report them.

### Journey

1. **Finds "Report a Sighting"** — in the "More" nav dropdown. Not in the primary nav. Discovery friction exists.
2. **Reads the form header:** "Anonymous, no account, no contact info required." ✅ Reassuring. The green checkmark note "Nothing is collected about you. No IP address is logged" is visible and credible.
3. **Camera type selection:** Six options presented as large tap-targets with descriptions. Well-designed. The "Unknown / Unsure" option is thoughtful — lowers barrier to submission. ✅
4. **Location fields:** Street address + city + state. No zip code, no coordinates. **No map picker.** For someone who spotted a camera while driving, they may not remember the exact address — they remember "somewhere on Highway 528 near the Walmart." A map-based pin-drop would dramatically improve accuracy and usability.
5. **State field:** Plain text input, max 2 chars, auto-uppercase. No dropdown. Risk: users type "Nm" → auto-corrects to "NM" ✅ (the code does `.toUpperCase()`). But no validation for valid US state abbreviations — someone types "US" or "CA" for Canada and it submits.
6. **Notes field:** Optional, good placeholder text ("Vendor branding visible? Mounted on pole or building? Facing which direction?"). ✅ Excellent guidance.
7. **Submit:** Button is disabled until all required fields filled — clear visual state (grey → red). ✅
8. **Success state:** "Your report is in the moderation queue. Once reviewed, it will be added to the surveillance map and tagged as community-reported." Two CTAs: "Report Another" and "View the Map."
9. **"View the Map"** → goes to /map. The spotter wants to see their submission. **But there's no way to find it.** The success message says "tagged as community-reported" but gives no submission ID, no confirmation of what was submitted, no timeline for review. The user has no way to tell if their sighting appeared.

### Friction Points

- **No map pin input.** Text address entry only. Camera spotters know location visually, not always by address. This probably reduces submission quality and completeness.
- **"Report a Sighting" hidden in "More" dropdown.** This is a primary community action. It should be in the primary nav or have a prominent homepage CTA.
- **No state validation.** Accepts any 2-letter string.
- **No photo upload.** The form accepts zero evidence. Contrast with the CampaignModal verify bounty which explicitly requires "GPS-tagged photo" — the sighting form accepts pure text with no verification mechanism. This is a quality gap.
- **No submission tracking.** After submitting, you cannot check on your submission. No reference ID, no "pending" status. If moderation takes weeks, the user has no way to know.
- **"In the moderation queue" — how long?** No SLA given. No indication of typical review time.
- **Error state:** Generic "Submission failed. Please try again in a moment." No detail on what failed, whether the data was saved, or how to try differently. If `ai.citeback.com` is down, users lose their submission silently.
- **No photo/media attachment.** Critical omission for a sighting report tool. A blurry photo of a Flock Safety unit is better evidence than a text description.
- **The success modal says "tagged as community-reported"** but the map currently shows the EFF Atlas data as the baseline. A user looking at the map after submitting would have no idea where community-reported cameras appear vs. the pre-loaded dataset.

### Can they track their submission?

**No.** Zero feedback loop after submission.

### Overall Impression: **Moderately satisfied but confused about what happens next**

The form itself is clean and the privacy messaging is strong. But the lack of photo upload, map picker, and any post-submission tracking path makes it feel like shouting into a void.

## Persona 5: The Journalist

> Researching a story on ALPR surveillance in NM. Looking for credibility, methodology, contact info.

### Journey

1. **Who runs this?** Checks footer, about page (doesn't exist), nav. Finds "Est. 2026 · New Mexico · United States" in the hero dateline. **No named individual, no organization, no press contact.**
2. **Data sources:** Footer has good attribution — EFF Atlas of Surveillance, OpenStreetMap, CourtListener, USASpending.gov, OpenStates, Congress.gov, Senate LDA. ✅ Solid provenance list.
3. **Methodology:** No methodology page. The map shows 92,000 cameras but there's no explanation of how the EFF data was ingested, how fresh it is, or how community-reported sightings are verified before appearing.
4. **Intelligence Feed / SurveillanceFeed:** (Lazy-loaded, not reviewed in code). Exists as `/intelligence-feed`. Journalist goes there.
5. **Governance doc:** Available at `/governance`. A journalist who reads it will be impressed by specificity — voting formulas, reputation caps, time-locks, platform fees. But they'll also notice "Wyoming DAO LLC (in formation)" and conclude there is no legal accountability entity yet.
6. **GitHub repo:** Linked. If populated, this is the journalist's best credibility signal. If sparse, it undermines the whole story.
7. **"Contact" link:** **Does not exist.** There is no contact page, no press email, no Nostr pubkey for reaching the team. A journalist literally cannot reach Citeback for comment or comment solicitation.
8. **"Activity Ticker"** — visible as a strip above the hero (ActivityTicker component). Shows live/recent platform activity. This is a credibility signal but also opaque — where does this data come from? Is it real?
9. **Transparency page** (`/transparency`): Exists. Not reviewed in detail but present — a journalist would find this useful.

### Friction Points

- **No press contact.** This is a journalism-ending friction point. A story without a comment from the platform operator is incomplete. The deliberate anonymity is a feature for donors but a bug for press coverage.
- **No methodology document** for the map data. Journalists can't cite a map without knowing methodology.
- **Data freshness unknown.** When was the EFF Atlas data last synced? How often does the platform update? No timestamp or "last updated" displayed.
- **"92,000 cameras" — where does this number come from?** If a journalist cites it, they need a source. The homepage number appears in the hero stats but has no footnote.
- **Wyoming DAO LLC not formed.** Any journalist will note "the entity doesn't exist yet" — that's a significant credibility caveat.
- **No named expert or founding team.** The Expert Directory (`/expert-directory`) exists but is for privacy/legal professionals, not the Citeback team itself.

### Overall Impression: **Skeptical of credibility — cannot use as primary source yet**

The data sourcing is good, the design is professional, the technical architecture is interesting. But no contact, no named team, no legal entity, and no methodology documentation make it uncitable as a source and unreachable for comment.

## Cross-Cutting Analysis

### First 10 Seconds: What Does the Hero Communicate?

**What lands:**
- "The surveillance state watches everyone. We fund the people fighting back." — Clear mission after reveal.
- Visual design is sharp, editorial, credible-looking. Not a scam aesthetic.
- "Pre-Launch" label appears in the eyebrow — sets expectations.

**What fails:**
- The **redaction animation** forces users to wait or interact before reading the headline. On slow connections or fast scrollers, the headline may never fully register.
- The **sub-paragraph leads with Monero/Zano** — crypto jargon in the second sentence of the primary value prop is a significant newcomer filter.
- **Platform stats sidebar shows $0 raised** — this is visually prominent and undermines credibility before the user understands context.
- **No single clear CTA dominates.** "See Campaigns" and "Run a Campaign" are equal weight. For 90% of visitors, "Run a Campaign" is irrelevant — it's noise.
- The **hero micro-text** ("No wallets activate until 10 public prerequisites...") is 12px opacity-0.7 — functionally invisible. This is the most trust-building sentence on the page and almost nobody reads it.

### Mobile Experience

**What works:**
- `.hero-grid` collapses to single column at 768px ✅
- `.hero-sidebar` gains top border instead of left border ✅  
- Footer padding reduces from 64px to 32px ✅
- Nav shows hamburger menu at 820px breakpoint ✅
- SightingForm uses `clamp()` for padding ✅
- Touch handlers for redaction reveal implemented ✅

**What breaks or degrades:**

1. **Nav breakpoint mismatch:** Desktop nav hides at `max-width: 820px`. But the "More" dropdown button is in the desktop nav — on tablet/mid-size screens (769–820px), the "More" dropdown disappears into the mobile hamburger menu. The campaign CTA "Fund a Campaign" moves to the bottom of the mobile menu where users may not scroll to find it.

2. **TrustFAQ trust pillars:** Use `repeat(auto-fit, minmax(200px, 1fr))`. On a 375px iPhone, this forces a single column of 6 cards — the section becomes very long and users may not read all pillars.

3. **Campaign modal:** `maxHeight: '92vh'` with `overflowY: auto` — this works, but on small phones (667px height), the modal is nearly full-screen and the close button may be out of thumb reach depending on where the user taps to open.

4. **CampaignCard share button:** 36x36px minimum — borderline for fat-thumb accuracy on mobile.

5. **SightingForm city/state grid:** `gridTemplateColumns: '2fr 1fr'` — fine on mobile, but the state field is narrow (1fr of ~360px screen = ~120px). With 2-char max length this is actually fine, but visually it looks cramped.

6. **Hero headline font:** `clamp(32px, 5.5vw, 76px)` — on 375px, this renders at ~20px (`5.5vw`), which is actually smaller than the floor. Wait — `5.5vw` of 375px = 20.6px. The `clamp` minimum is 32px, so the minimum wins. This is fine. ✅

7. **ActivityTicker:** Not reviewed in code, but if it uses horizontal scroll or animation, this is a common mobile performance issue.

8. **Lazy-loaded components (CameraMap, SurveillanceFeed, ConversationalInterface):** The map in particular will have mobile performance challenges — a Leaflet/MapLibre tile map on a low-end mobile device with 92k data points will likely lag or crash.

### Error States

**SightingForm API failure:**
```jsx
setError(true) → "Submission failed. Please try again in a moment."
```
- Verdict: **Minimal but functional.** The error is shown, not swallowed. But:
  - No indication of whether the submission was partially saved
  - No retry mechanism — user must manually resubmit
  - No guidance on alternatives (e.g., "submit via GitHub issue")
  - The data is not persisted client-side — if the user refreshes after an error, they lose everything they typed

**ConversationalInterface (AI Chat) failure:**
- The component is lazy-loaded. If `ai.citeback.com` is unreachable, the component may fail to load entirely with the generic `<LazyFallback label="Loading AI…" />`. No error state for the AI being down specifically.
- The AI button is a fixed floating element. If loading fails silently, the button stays visible but the chat is broken — users get a spinner that never resolves.

**CampaignModal interest signal failure:**
- The `handleInterest` function has a catch block that increments the count locally anyway: `setInterestCount(c => c + 1)`. This creates a **false positive** — the user believes their interest was recorded when the server request failed. Optimistic UI without reconciliation.

**Network offline:**
- No offline handling anywhere in the app. Standard React/Vite SPA behavior — it breaks silently.

### Empty States

**Map with 92k cameras but 0 community sightings:**

This is the platform's biggest conceptual confusion point.

- Users visiting `/map` see tens of thousands of camera pins. Impressive and alarming.
- They don't know: are these all from the EFF? Are any community-reported? Is this real-time or from 2022?
- A new user who submits a sighting via the form will navigate to the map looking for their pin. **There is no way to distinguish their community-submitted pin from the pre-loaded EFF data.**
- No legend or data source layer toggle is visible from the code (may exist inside `CameraMap.jsx` which wasn't reviewed).
- The "92k cameras" stat in the hero is from the pre-loaded dataset, not from Citeback's community. A user who thinks "92k cameras tracked by Citeback's community" is making a fundamentally wrong assumption. This is a **credibility inflation risk** — if that misunderstanding becomes journalism, it becomes a liability.

**Campaigns at 0% with no donation path:**

Every campaign shows an empty progress bar and "Wallet pending." This is correct for pre-launch, but without context the page reads as:
- Either: "This platform never works" (bounce)
- Or: "Something is broken" (confusion)

The "Pre-Launch" badge on cards is present but subtle. The explanation for *why* wallets are pending requires clicking into a card and reading the modal. Most users won't.

### Trust Signals

**Strong trust signals:**
- Sharp, editorial visual design — feels authoritative, not scammy
- Detailed, honest TrustFAQ with risk disclosures (TEE attacks, OFAC gap, regulatory uncertainty)
- Infrastructure transparency (Netlify/Bunny CDN/CARTO disclosure)
- GitHub repo links throughout
- Fourth Amendment quote in hero sidebar — grounds the mission in law
- Data attribution footer — EFF, OpenStreetMap, CourtListener, Congress.gov etc.
- Phase 1 banner — honest about what doesn't exist yet
- "All donations final and non-refundable" disclosed in footer (legally appropriate)
- Interest count on campaign cards — social proof (if non-zero)

**Trust-undermining signals:**
- **$0 raised, everywhere, all the time** — correct but damaging
- **"4/10 prerequisites cleared"** in hero stats with no link or tooltip
- **Anonymous founder** with no legal entity yet formed
- **Wyoming DAO LLC "(in formation)"** — the legal accountability mechanism doesn't exist
- **TEE architecture described as present-tense** in hero and nav ("keys are sealed inside a TEE enclave") when it's actually future-state
- **CampaignModal optimistic interest counter bug** — if discovered, undermines trust
- **No .onion address** for a privacy-resistance platform
- **Bunny CDN for fonts** — privacy activists notice this
- **No PGP or Nostr contact** — no secure way to reach the team

### Call to Action Clarity: What Is the ONE Thing?

**The platform does not have a single primary CTA.** It has at least five competing actions:
1. See Campaigns (donate eventually)
2. Run a Campaign (become an operator)
3. Report a Sighting (contribute data)
4. Ask Citeback AI (conversational)
5. View the Map (browse)

For a pre-launch platform that cannot yet accept donations, the primary CTA should probably be: **"Signal interest in campaigns so we know what to prioritize at launch."** But that's not framed that way anywhere. "Signal Interest" is buried inside a campaign modal, not presented as the platform's primary engagement mechanism.

The floating "💬 Ask Citeback AI" button is actually the most discoverable interactive element on the page — but it's not framed as the entry point. For a newcomer who doesn't know what to do, being greeted by "Ask me about surveillance" could be the most natural onboarding flow. It's currently an afterthought UI element.

## Priority Recommendations

### P0 — Fix These Before Any Launch Announcement

1. **Add a launch timeline.** "Phase 2 — Q3 2026" or a real date. The absence of any timeline turns "pre-launch" into "indefinite vaporware" for skeptical users.

2. **Surface Frontline Funds prominently.** This is the only current path to action for motivated donors. It needs to be in the primary nav or on the hero — not buried in "More."

3. **Fix the "TEE is live" language tense.** The hero says "wallet keys are sealed inside a TEE enclave" — present tense. This is false today. Change to "will be sealed" or "at launch, sealed."

4. **Add a map legend / data source indicator.** Users need to know: "These 92k cameras are from EFF Atlas (last updated X). Community sightings appear as [different marker color]."

5. **Sighting form: show submission status.** Give users a reference ID. Tell them the typical review time. Persist form data locally so an error doesn't lose their input.

### P1 — Before Wallets Go Live

6. **Add a photo/media attachment to the sighting form.** Text-only reports are low quality. Even a single photo field would improve data significantly.

7. **Add a map pin picker to the sighting form.** "Show us where" is more intuitive than "type an address."

8. **Re-frame "Signal Interest" CTA.** It should read: "Notify me at launch" or "I'll fund this when it's live" — not a thumbs-up that goes nowhere.

9. **Create a press/contact page.** Even if anonymous, a Nostr pubkey or ProtonMail contact allows journalists and researchers to reach out. The current total absence kills earned media.

10. **Address the $0 context.** Add a small explainer on the campaigns list: "Wallets aren't live yet — this is the pre-launch queue. [Learn why →]"

### P2 — Polish and Trust

11. **Self-host fonts** (swap Bunny CDN for bundled fonts). Privacy platform serving fonts from a CDN is ideologically inconsistent.

12. **Fix optimistic interest counter.** If the API call fails, don't increment the displayed count. Or confirm with a reconciliation step.

13. **Add state/territory validation** to the sighting form.

14. **Make the hero micro-text visible.** The "No wallets activate until 10 public prerequisites are verified" sentence is your best trust sentence. Make it readable — at least 14px, full opacity.

15. **Consider a .onion address.** Not required, but a surveillance-resistance platform without one will be called out by technically-oriented users and journalists.
